Primary Research

Streamflow Prediction via Mass-Conserving LSTMs

Status: Master's Thesis (Manuscript under review)
Domain: Hydrological Modelling & Deep Learning
Metric: Dual-State Physical Consistency

Research Scope

Rainfall-runoff modeling is a foundational challenge in hydrology, key to flood mitigation, water resource allocation, and climate resilience planning. While standard Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks excel at streamflow forecasting, they are fundamentally unconstrained, meaning they violate core conservation of mass principles by occasionally predicting more run-off discharge than available rainfall precipitation. This thesis presents a physically consistent, mass-conserving LSTM architecture utilizing dual internal states.

Technical Implementation

The research builds on physics-informed neural network structures, blending physical hydrology formulations directly into deep neural architecture layers:

  • Dual State Representation: Configured cell architectures to segment states into physical water storage reservoirs and hidden routing delays.
  • Conservation Bottlenecks: Constrained internal recurrence routing coefficients so that physical mass balances (Precipitation = Runoff + Evapotranspiration + Storage Accumulation) are mathematically maintained.
  • Evaluation Benchmarks: Assessed architecture performance across massive catchment datasets, validating both physical mass balance compliance and general prediction indicators.

Significance

By establishing physical consistency without sacrificing predictive capability, this dual-state mass-conserving framework provides environmental scientists and planners with highly reliable forecasting outputs, mitigating risk in regional water infrastructure management.
